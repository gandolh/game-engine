The candidate

rubberduck's isometric medieval buildings — genuinely CC0, and genuinely the right shape

Citadel renders 2:1 dimetric on a 32×16 tile, authoring building art at ISO_ART_SCALE = 2. Its real building frames run 64×62 (the well) to 192×186 (the keep), typically 128×92. The pack's “64×32” variant trims to about 255×269 and downscales cleanly into that box. Geometry was never the blocker — which is exactly why it was worth testing properly rather than dismissing.

The catch that matters is upstream of any of that: these are photoreal Blender renders, not pixel art. That single fact is what the two experiments below independently ran into.

Experiment 1 — nearest-colour

Quantizing to the nearest EDG32 swatch turns every building to rust

The obvious approach, and the one the original plan assumed: map each pixel to its closest EDG32 colour using a weighted RGB distance. Dithering was tried as a rescue.

Experiment 2 — material ramps

A smarter restyle fixes the hue story and still fails

Second attempt, designed specifically to dodge the first failure: classify each pixel into a material (roof, timber, plaster, stone, foliage) by hue and saturation, then map its luminance onto a hand-picked EDG32 ramp for that material. Colour is no longer chosen by proximity, so it cannot snap to rust by accident.

Why — the actual mechanism

EDG32 has no warm neutral. The renders are made of almost nothing else.

Plotting the source art's 60 heaviest colours against all 32 palette entries shows the problem geometrically. Saturation runs up the vertical axis; luminance runs across.

source render colours (60 heaviest) EDG32 swatches

EDG32 contains exactly two low-saturation mid-tone swatches — #c0cbdc and #8b9bb4 — and both are cool blue-greys. The palette has no warm neutral whatsoever. Its warm family leaps straight from a near-black brown (#733e39) to saturated rusts (#b86f50, #be4a2f). A photoreal render's weathered timber and grey-brown thatch land squarely in the hole between them, so the nearest available colour is always a rust.

All 32 EDG32 swatches. Ringed: the only two neutral mid-tones — both cool.

This is a gamut and texture-frequency mismatch, not a tuning bug. EDG32 is a vivid, high-contrast palette that expects flat, deliberate colour fields with intentional dithering. Photographic texture forced into 32 vivid colours at 128 px yields speckle no matter which mapping function you choose. There is no third experiment worth running.

The CC0 isometric-medieval ecosystem is dominated by rendered art. Limited-palette isometric pixel art in this genre — the only kind that would convert near-losslessly — is scarce. Your locked “no external art pipeline” decision holds, and no EDG32 amendment is needed.

What the spike found instead

Eight of Citadel's 21 buildings are the same box

Baking the current recipes to a contact sheet to compare against the imports turned up a defect worth more than the imports were.

That contradicts the file's own stated goal. buildings.ts claims each type uses “a distinct FORM … so the silhouette — not just the colour — tells a mill from a mine.” For those eight, it doesn't. Only mill, chapel, keep, tower, garrison, town-hall, well and farm currently read at a glance.

Colour is also the worst axis to lean on in this engine, because the day/night wash tints everything — at dusk a red-roofed bakery and an orange-roofed house converge into the same shape. And iso-draw.ts already exposes exactly the primitives needed to fix it, currently unused by these types:

  • isoAnvil, isoChimney — a forge canopy and an oven bulge
  • isoLogPile, isoChoppingBlock, isoWaterWheel — sawmill and woodcutter
  • isoQuarryPit, isoShaftMouth — let the pit dominate the read, not the hut
  • isoBanner, isoTurret, isoCrates — square, watchpost, market

So the brief now proposes silhouette differentiation, gated on a colour-stripped silhouette test, with the CC0 renders kept as visual reference only — no pixels ingested, no licence obligations, no decision amended.
